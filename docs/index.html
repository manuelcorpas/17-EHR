🧠 NTDs Are Structurally Isolated in Research

17 WHO Neglected Tropical Diseases show significantly higher semantic isolation than other diseases:

  • Mean SII: 0.00242 vs 0.00201 (p=0.002, Cohen's d=0.82)
  • Lower Knowledge Transfer Potential (p=0.009)
  • Guinea worm disease has the highest isolation (2x mean)

This isolation limits NTDs' ability to benefit from advances in related fields, compounding existing funding gaps.

Semantic Metrics Explained

SII (Semantic Isolation Index)

Measures how far a disease's research is from the centroid of all biomedical knowledge. Higher values indicate more isolated, siloed research.

Interpretation: Diseases with high SII (top 25%) are research "islands" disconnected from mainstream science

KTP (Knowledge Transfer Potential)

Maximum similarity to any other disease. Lower values mean less opportunity to benefit from advances in related fields.

Interpretation: Most diseases have high KTP (>99%), but even small differences indicate reduced spillover benefit

RCC (Research Clustering Coefficient)

Density of connections in the knowledge network. Lower values indicate sparser research communities.

Interpretation: Low RCC diseases have fewer collaborating research fields and less integrated knowledge

Unified Score (0-100)

Combined neglect score integrating Discovery (biobank gaps), Translation (trial equity), and Knowledge (semantic isolation).

Interpretation: Higher scores = more neglected. Top diseases score 40+ across all three dimensions

💡 Key Insight: The Pipeline Paradox

While geographic concentration is better in clinical trials (2.5:1 vs 57.8:1 HIC:LMIC ratio), disease priorities are worse. Cancer research dominates trials even more than biobank publications (61.5% vs 42%), suggesting that while trials are more geographically accessible, research agendas remain skewed toward high-income country priorities.

What This Means

The three-dimensional analysis reveals that research inequity is not random but structural. The same diseases that are neglected in one dimension tend to be neglected across all dimensions, suggesting systemic factors that perpetuate the gap.

Addressing this requires interventions at multiple levels: funding priorities, trial site selection, and deliberate efforts to connect isolated research communities to mainstream biomedical science.

About HEIM Framework

Methodology, data sources, and how to interpret this dashboard

🎯 What is HEIM?

HEIM (Health Equity Informative Metrics) is a comprehensive framework for measuring alignment between biomedical research output and global health priorities. It quantifies the "discovery-to-translation gap"-showing how health equity disparities persist and even worsen as research moves from basic science (biobanks) to clinical application (trials).

The framework answers critical questions:

  • Are we studying the diseases that cause the most suffering?
  • Do Global South populations benefit equitably from research?
  • Is the situation improving or worsening over time?

📊 Data Sources

Clinical Trials Data (Translation)

Trial data comes from ClinicalTrials.gov via the AACT database (Aggregate Analysis of ClinicalTrials.gov).

  • 563,725 studies retrieved (2000-2025)
  • 2,189,930 disease-trial mappings (trials can address multiple diseases)
  • 770,178 trial site records across 194 countries

Disease Burden Data

Burden data from Global Burden of Disease Study 2021 (IHME).

  • DALYs (Disability-Adjusted Life Years): One DALY = one year of healthy life lost
  • 89 disease categories mapped to GBD Level 3 causes
  • 29 Global South Priority diseases identified based on WHO criteria and LMIC burden distribution

Biobank Data (Discovery)

Publication data from PubMed for 70 biobanks in the IHCC registry.

PubMed Semantic Data (Knowledge)

Abstracts retrieved from PubMed (2000-2025) using MeSH terms and keyword searches. Embeddings generated using PubMedBERT.

  • 13,100,113 papers retrieved for 176 diseases
  • 13,100,113 embeddings generated (768-dimensional dense vectors)
  • 176 diseases with sufficient data for analysis

📐 Key Metrics Explained

Research Intensity

Research Intensity = Trials (or Publications) / Million DALYs

Measures research attention relative to disease burden. Higher = more research per unit of suffering.

Intensity Gap

Intensity Gap = Non-GS Intensity / GS Priority Intensity

Ratio of research intensity between non-Global South diseases and GS Priority diseases. A value of 2.4x means non-GS diseases receive 2.4 times more research attention per DALY.

Global South Priority Diseases

29 diseases identified based on WHO classification criteria and GBD 2021 burden distribution:

  • WHO Neglected Tropical Diseases: Chagas, Dengue, Leishmaniasis, Lymphatic filariasis, Malaria, Onchocerciasis, Schistosomiasis, Trachoma, etc.
  • Major infectious diseases: TB, HIV/AIDS, Lower respiratory infections, Diarrheal diseases, Meningitis
  • Maternal and neonatal: Maternal disorders, Neonatal disorders
  • Nutritional: Protein-energy malnutrition, Dietary iron deficiency

Selection based on diseases where >60% of global DALYs occur in low- and middle-income countries.

Semantic Isolation Index (SII)

SII = mean(cosine_distance(disease_embedding, global_centroid))

Measures how far a disease's research is from the center of all biomedical knowledge. Higher SII indicates more isolated research that doesn't benefit from mainstream advances.

Knowledge Transfer Potential (KTP)

KTP = max(cosine_similarity(disease_i, disease_j)) for all j ≠ i

Maximum similarity to any other disease. Higher KTP means the disease can potentially benefit from advances in related fields.

Unified Triple-Burden Score

Unified = 0.33×Discovery + 0.33×Translation + 0.34×Knowledge

Combines Gap Score (biobanks), Clinical Trial Equity, and Semantic Isolation into a single measure of compound neglect. Higher scores indicate diseases facing disadvantage across multiple dimensions.

Weighting justification: Equal weights are used because the three dimensions are empirically orthogonal (inter-dimension correlation r=0.07), meaning they capture independent aspects of neglect rather than redundant signals. This orthogonality supports treating them as equally important contributors to overall disadvantage.

🎯 Policy Implications

The identification of semantic isolation as a measurable dimension of neglect suggests actionable interventions:

  • Targeted funding mechanisms: Prioritize semantically isolated diseases (high SII) for cross-disciplinary research initiatives
  • Research network building: Fund collaborative projects that explicitly connect NTD researchers with mainstream biomedical science
  • WHO monitoring: Consider adopting SII as a complementary indicator alongside traditional R&D metrics
  • Knowledge translation: Develop systematic reviews that explicitly bridge isolated disease literature with related therapeutic areas

The finding that isolation is measurable means progress can be tracked - providing accountability for equity-focused research investments.

⚠ Limitations

  • Data sources: ClinicalTrials.gov has US/Western registration bias; PubMed has English-language bias
  • Biobank coverage: 70 IHCC biobanks may not represent all global biobank activity
  • Temporal scope: Embeddings trained on 2000-2025 literature; historical patterns may differ
  • Generalizability: The NTD finding is robust; broader framework applicability requires further validation
